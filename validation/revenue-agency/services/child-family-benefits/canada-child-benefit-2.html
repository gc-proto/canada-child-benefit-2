Who can apply

Who can get the CCB

You must meet all of the following conditions:

  • have a child that is under 18 years of age
  • the child lives with you all the time, or at least about half of the time (see Custody arrangements and your benefits)
  • be primarily responsible for the care and upbringing of the child
  • be a resident of Canada for tax purposes

In addition, you or your spouse or common-law partner must be any of the following:

  • a Canadian citizen
  • a permanent resident
  • a protected person (refugee)
  • a temporary resident who has lived in Canada for the previous 18 months, and who has a valid permit in the 19th month
  • an Indigenous person who meets the definition of "Indian" under the Indian Act

You are not eligible for the CCB if the child is legally, physically, or financially maintained by a child welfare agency. For more information on this situation, see children's special allowances.

Who is primarily responsible for the the care of the child

The person who is primarily responsible for the care and upbringing of the child should apply for the CCB. This means that you are responsible for things such as:

  • supervising the child’s daily activities and needs
  • making sure the child’s medical needs are met
  • arranging for child care when necessary

If there is a female parent who lives with the child she is usually considered to be primarily responsible or the care and upbringing of the child. She should be the one applying for CCB.

If there is a female parent who lives with the child but you want the male parent to receive the payment, go to Male parent is primarily responsible for hte upbringing of the child.

If the child lives with you only part of the time, you can still be considered primarily responsible for the care and upbringing of the child for the periods the child lives with you. Go to Custody arrangements and your benefits.

Custody arrangements and your benefits

If a child only lives with you part time, you need to determine if you are considered to have shared custody.

Determine if you are in shared custody situation

Does the child spend their time:

  • about equally between both parents

    If the child shares its time about equally between the parents, then both parents are considered to have shared custody of that child:

    • each parent must apply for CCB separately
    • the amount each parent will get is calculated separately, using their respective family income
    • each parent will get exactly 50% of what they would have gotten if they had sole custody of the child
    • parents can't ask CRA to split the amount differently, or give the the full amount to one of the parent
  • mostly with you

    If the child lives with you most of the time, you are considered to have sole custody:

    • only you can apply for CCB
    • your payment will be calculated based on your net family income (not taking into account the other parent's income)
    • you can't ask CRA to split the amount differently between you and the other parent
  • mostly with the other parent

    If the child lives with the other parent most of the time, the other parent is considered to have sole custody:

    • only the other parent can apply for CCB
    • their payment will be calculated based on the other parent's net family income (not taking into account your income)
    • you can't ask CRA to split the amount differently between you and the other parent

If you start or end a shared custody situation, let us know that your situation changed

Temporary change of custody

Changes in custody can sometimes be temporary. This can happen, for example, if the child usually lives with one parent, but goes to live with the other parent for the summer.

In these cases, the parent that gets custody on a temporary basis can apply to get CCB payments for that period. When the child returns to live with the other parent, that parent needs to reapply to start getting the payments again.
